# AI Blind Demo Panel

## Executive Summary

* Total simulated participants: 12
* Average comprehension score: 4.1 / 5
* Average choice clarity score: 3.7 / 5
* Average question clarity score: 3.7 / 5
* Average report relevance score: 4.3 / 5
* Average trust score: 3.5 / 5
* Average AI-smell risk: 2.5 / 5
* Average payment intent: 3.3 / 5
* Most common confusion: The 7-Day option feels more precise, but several users need a simpler explanation of what the One-Time result already gives them.
* Most common trust-builder: Clear professional-check language, privacy reassurance, and examples of how the diary improves the map.
* Most common payment blocker: Users want to see a sample report or a concrete outcome before paying, especially for the 7-Day option.
* Priority fixes before real human demo: Sharpen the One-Time vs 7-Day comparison, add one short sample-result preview, make professional-check copy reassuring rather than medical, and keep the 7-Day CTA framed as refinement instead of pressure.

## Cross-Participant Findings

### 1. Landing comprehension

Most participants understood that the product maps eating and weight patterns rather than giving a generic diet plan. The clearest language was about patterns, defaults, triggers, and leverage points. Lower-comprehension users needed a simpler sentence that says the product finds the situation where weight-loss attempts usually break.

### 2. One-Time vs 7-Day clarity

One-Time feels like a low-risk entry point and is attractive to skeptical, privacy-sensitive, or impatient users. 7-Day feels worth more when users understand that the diary captures real-life variation, not just extra tracking. The comparison should state that One-Time is complete enough to buy, while 7-Day refines the map with observed days.

### 3. Question wording

Concrete timing, sleep, meal rhythm, social, and default-choice questions were easiest. Emotion and body-visibility questions were valuable but sensitive. For low-emotion users, the product needs a practical route that does not require therapy-like language.

### 4. Report quality

Report relevance was the strongest simulated signal. The best moments came when reports named a precise life context: after children sleep, late work fatigue, fasting rebound, social weekends, night cravings, or private body anxiety. Generic wellness phrasing is the main report-quality risk.

### 5. Safety/trust

Professional-check copy increased trust for cautious participants when it was calm and plain. It can scare users if it sounds like diagnosis, medical judgment, or an urgent warning without context. The product should keep saying it is a self-reflection and pattern-mapping tool.

### 6. Payment intent

Payment intent is moderate-positive. Users are most willing to pay when the report feels specific and when they can preview the kind of output they will receive. One-Time is the best first purchase for skeptics; 7-Day is strongest for busy, stress, sleep, and health-boundary users.

### 7. Pricing signals

* One-Time Deep Assessment: 4,900-9,900 MNT feels plausible.
* 7-Day Deep Assessment: 14,900-29,000 MNT feels plausible when diary value is clear.
* Upgrade from One-Time to 7-Day: A discounted difference should be tested because several users prefer proving relevance with One-Time first.
* 29,000 MNT+ needs stronger proof, such as a sample report, expert framing, or a high-detail 7-Day output.

### 8. AI-smell risks

The main AI-smell risk is not the concept; it is generic encouragement, general wellness language, and advice that is not visibly tied to the user's answers. Report copy should keep using concrete life-context evidence and avoid sounding like a broad habit article.

### 9. Recommended fixes

1. Add a short sample-result preview before payment or product choice.
2. Clarify that One-Time is a complete snapshot and 7-Day is a refinement layer.
3. Show that diary entries can be short and imperfect.
4. Add low-emotion wording for default, delivery, gaming, and social users.
5. Keep body-shame and health-context copy privacy-first, neutral, and non-diagnostic.
6. Add one sentence explaining why sleep, caffeine, and energy rhythm are part of the map.
7. Keep the 7-Day CTA framed as a natural refinement, not a pushy upsell.

## Decision

* Ready for human demo: Yes, after light copy polish.
* Needs copy polish first: Yes. Prioritize product-choice comparison, diary effort reassurance, sample-result preview, and privacy/safety boundary copy.
* Needs pricing/paywall test first: Yes, but only after the comparison copy is clearer. Test One-Time at 6,900-9,900 MNT and 7-Day at 19,900 MNT with an upgrade discount.
* Needs shorter assessment flow first: Not globally. Add a faster low-emotion route or clearer skip tolerance, but do not shorten the full flow until real users show consistent drop-off.

The AI panel supports moving toward a small human demo after copy polish. The strongest product signal is report relevance. The largest business risk is asking users to pay before they can see what kind of personalized output they will get.
